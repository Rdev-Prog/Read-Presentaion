The Tell Tale Heart

By: Edgar Allan Poe

In this classic psychological thriller, the reader will find many more questions than answers. Even though this is one of Poe's shortest stories, nevertheless it has become one of his most highest regarded works. It is a profound and, at times, ambiguous investigation of the paranoia that may lie within the depths of one man's mind...

Where did it happen?

The setting of the story creates a tense atmosphere which highlights the narrator's psychological torment. While the story does not explicitly state that it takes place in the old man's house, there are hints to this, particularly in scenes set in the old man's bedroom. The majority of the events take place every night when the narrator looks at the old man while sleeping, which is also when the murder takes place.

Who are they?

The protagonist and narrator of Edgar Allan Poe's The Tell-Tale Heart is an unnamed killer who has a mental illness. that became obsessed and fixated on the "vulture eyes of his victim," which they perceived as evil. The antagonist is the old man who is depicted as frail and unaware of the protagonist's hatred with his eyes. He became a victim not because of his actions but by his physical appearance, which made his death senseless and tragic. The supporting characters are the neighbor and officers. The neighbor heard a noise from the old man's house that made them call the officers. The officers went to the house and didn't suspect anything, but the protagonist went mad and admitted the killing.

How did it start?

The Tell-Tale Heart by Edgar Allan Poe explores the story of a mentally unstable individual consumed by paranoia and obsession. The narrator focuses on defining the old man who has "vulture eyes," claiming that it's evil that leads to planning and committing murder. The narrator also explained how detailed they observed the old man for a week and executed the crime on the 8th day, explaining how the killer killed the old man, dismembering the old parts, and how the killer cleaned the crime scene, ensuring that there's no trace of the crime, and hid the body parts below the wooden plank. As the police officers came to investigate the disturbance, the killer talked to them without showing any remorse or fear from what they had done with the old man, but as they continued to talk, the killer heard the sound of the old man's heartbeat. The killer began to panic in his mind. Later on, eaten by guilt, the killer confessed the crime to the police officers.

What builds the tension?

The rising action begins when the narrator becomes so obsessed with the old man's eyes that he refers to them as the "vulture eye". This leads him to plan the murder of the old man to get rid of his eyes. Every night for a week, the narrator sneaks into the room of the old man to observe what the old man does in a pitch dark room and make sure that he will not wake up the old man. The tension builds as the narrator grows more paranoid on the eye and being more and more anxious about murdering the old man.

What is the intense part?

The climax of "The Tell-Tale Heart" occurs when the narrator, overwhelmed by the relentless sound of the old man's heartbeat, loses control. He suffocates the old man with his mattress in a moment of insanity. The sound of the heartbeat, which had previously tormented him, continues to haunt him even after the murder.
